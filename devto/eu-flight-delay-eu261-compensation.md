A flight delayed several hours by a technical fault often ends with an airline handing out a meal voucher and a rebooking, as if that fully discharges their obligation. It doesn't. Regulation (EC) No 261/2004 is widely regarded as the most aggressive air passenger protection law in the world, and it entitles you to direct cash compensation, entirely separate from any meal or accommodation assistance you receive at the airport.

## The 3-hour threshold and what triggers it

If your flight arrives at its final destination 3 hours or more later than scheduled, and the cause was within the airline's control, you're entitled to fixed cash compensation — no separate proof of financial loss required, and independent of what you paid for your ticket.

## The compensation tiers, by distance

The amount owed scales with the flight's distance, not the ticket price: €250 for flights up to 1,500 km; €400 for flights between 1,500 and 3,500 km (and for longer intra-EU flights); €600 for flights over 3,500 km. These figures are fixed by the regulation itself, not discretionary.

## The "extraordinary circumstances" defence — and its real limits

Airlines can avoid paying only if they can demonstrate the delay was caused by extraordinary circumstances genuinely outside their control — severe weather, air traffic control strikes unrelated to the airline's own staff, or airspace closures. Critically, a technical fault with the aircraft or a crew shortage does not qualify as extraordinary — these are treated as ordinary operational risks of running an airline, and courts across the EU have consistently upheld this distinction.

## Building your evidence at the gate

Photograph the departure boards showing the delay. Ask ground staff for the official reason for the delay in writing before boarding — a verbal explanation given after the fact is much harder to rely on than something documented in the moment.

## Filing directly, not through a claims agency

Third-party claims agencies typically take 25-40% of your compensation for handling the paperwork. For most cases, filing the EU's standard claim form directly with the airline costs nothing extra and keeps your full payout. Airlines face statutory deadlines to respond, and national civil aviation authorities — such as AESA in Spain or the DGAC in France — can be brought in if an airline stonewalls a legitimate claim.

## What doesn't count against you

Your right to compensation exists independently of any assistance (food, drink, communication) the airline provides during the wait — accepting a meal voucher doesn't waive your right to also claim the cash compensation once the delay is confirmed.
