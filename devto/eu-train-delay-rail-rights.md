Rail delays across Europe are often assumed to be a dead end for passengers — an inconvenience with no financial remedy attached, unlike air travel. That assumption is wrong. Regulation (EU) 2021/782 on rail passengers' rights and obligations gives you a genuine, tiered right to a cash refund when a train arrives significantly late, regardless of the operator.

## The 60-minute threshold

If your train arrives at its final destination more than 60 minutes late, the rail operator — Renfe, SNCF, Deutsche Bahn, Eurostar, or any other covered by the regulation — is legally obligated to compensate you with a direct partial refund of your ticket price, paid in cash (or another method you choose) within one month of your claim.

## The compensation tiers

The refund scales with the length of delay upon arrival: 25% of the ticket price for delays between 60 and 119 minutes; 50% of the ticket price for delays of 120 minutes or more. Unlike EU261 for flights, this is a percentage of what you paid, not a flat fee.

## Where rail operators can avoid paying — and where they can't

Unlike airlines, rail operators are exempt from the cash compensation specifically if the delay was caused by genuine, unavoidable external force majeure — extreme weather events, people on the tracks, or sabotage, for example. What doesn't change under any circumstances, including force majeure, is the obligation to provide free food, drink, and overnight accommodation if you're stranded — that duty of care applies regardless of the cause of the delay.

## Getting your delay validated in real time

Ask the conductor or station staff to stamp or digitally validate your physical or electronic ticket as "delayed" while you're still travelling — this heads off disputes at the ticket counter later, where operators sometimes push back on claims lodged well after the fact without contemporaneous proof.

## Missed connections on through-tickets

If you're travelling on a combined or "through" ticket and miss your connecting train because of the first train's delay, the operator is legally obligated to reroute you free of charge on the next available train — even one operated by a different, partner rail company — or to cover an alternative form of transport, such as a bus or taxi, to keep your journey moving.

## Submitting your claim

Most operators run an online delay-compensation form directly on their website, generally requiring your ticket details and the actual arrival time versus the scheduled one. Keep your validated ticket and any station documentation as backup if the claim is initially disputed.
