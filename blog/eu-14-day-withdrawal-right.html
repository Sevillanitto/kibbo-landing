The 14-Day Right of Withdrawal in the EU: Why "No Refunds" Signs Don't Apply to Online Purchases

A lot of shoppers across the EU still believe that returning something you bought online is a courtesy the retailer extends, not a right the law guarantees. That belief is backwards, and it costs consumers real money every year. Under EU consumer law, distance purchases — anything bought online, by phone, or by mail order — come with a mandatory 14-day cooling-off period, and as of 19 June 2026, the mechanics of using that right have gotten dramatically easier thanks to a new legal requirement most shoppers haven't heard of yet.

The right itself: no reason required, no penalty allowed

The right of withdrawal, rooted in the Consumer Rights Directive (2011/83/EU), gives you 14 calendar days from the day you receive a physical product (or from the day you sign a service contract) to cancel the purchase entirely — without explaining why, and without the retailer imposing a cancellation fee. This applies regardless of what country within the EU the retailer is based in, as long as they're selling to consumers inside the Union.

What's new in 2026: the mandatory "withdrawal button"

Historically, the practical problem wasn't the existence of the right — it was exercising it. Retailers buried cancellation behind contact forms, unclear T&Cs, or support emails that went unanswered for days. Directive (EU) 2023/2673 closes that gap by adding a new Article 11a to the Consumer Rights Directive: as of 19 June 2026, online retailers selling into the EU must provide a clearly visible, dedicated withdrawal function directly on their site or account — commonly called the "withdrawal button." It has to work as a genuine two-step process: click to initiate withdrawal, then confirm with your order details, followed by an automatic confirmation sent to you on a durable medium such as email. Retailers can no longer bury this behind a support ticket queue or require a phone call — the point of the rule is explicit in the legislative text: withdrawing must not be harder than buying was in the first place.

This applies to any business selling goods, services, or digital content online to EU consumers, regardless of where that business is legally headquartered — a US or UK retailer selling into the EU is bound by this just as much as a company based in Berlin or Madrid.

What's excluded from the 14-day right

The withdrawal right isn't universal. Common exclusions include custom-made or personalised goods, perishable items like food, sealed goods that have been unsealed for hygiene reasons (think opened cosmetics or underwear), fixed-date travel bookings such as flights, trains, and hotel reservations, and digital content once you've expressly consented to immediate delivery and acknowledged you're giving up your withdrawal right by doing so (more on this in our digital content guide).

The 12-month penalty for retailers who don't inform you properly

One of the sharpest teeth in this law is aimed squarely at retailers who try to skip the paperwork. If a seller fails to clearly inform you, in writing, of your right of withdrawal before you complete the purchase, the standard 14-day window doesn't just extend a little — it extends automatically by a full 12 months. In practice, this means a retailer that quietly omits withdrawal information from checkout can find itself facing a valid return request nearly a year after the sale, at full refund value.

How to actually exercise it

From June 2026 onward, your first move should be the retailer's dedicated withdrawal button — check your account, order history, or order confirmation email for it. If a retailer hasn't implemented one yet (some smaller sellers lag behind large deadlines), you can still use the EU's standard model withdrawal form, or simply send an unambiguous written statement of your decision to withdraw. Once you've notified the seller within the 14-day window, you then have a further 14 days to physically return the goods.

Who pays for shipping

The retailer must refund the product price and the standard, ordinary delivery cost you originally paid — not necessarily an express or premium option you chose instead. The cost of shipping the item back to the retailer, however, falls on you unless the seller failed to clearly disclose in advance that you'd bear that cost, or unless the seller voluntarily offers free returns.
