Buying from Another EU Country: Your Cross-Border Rights and Geo-Blocking Defenses

If you've ever tried to buy something from an online store based in a different EU country and found yourself redirected to a local version with higher prices, or had your foreign-issued card mysteriously rejected, you've run into geo-blocking — a practice the EU has made explicitly illegal for most consumer transactions, though the rule has real limits worth understanding before you assume every case is a violation.

What geo-blocking actually is, and why it's banned

Regulation (EU) 2018/302 prohibits online traders from discriminating against consumers based on their nationality, place of residence, or the location of their payment account. In practical terms: a retailer in Germany can't legally charge you more, restrict your access, or refuse your payment method purely because you're accessing the site from Portugal, Greece, or any other EU member state — you're entitled to the same prices and purchase conditions as a local customer.

The important limit: access rights, not delivery obligations

This is where a lot of consumer guides oversimplify the regulation in a way that sets up disappointment. The rule guarantees you equal access, pricing, and payment terms — it does not force a retailer to physically ship to your country if that's genuinely outside their normal delivery zone. What the regulation does require, if a retailer won't ship to you directly, is that they still let you complete the purchase and either arrange your own collection from their local premises, or use your own cross-border courier or freight-forwarding service to have it picked up on your behalf. In other words: the seller can't block the sale itself, even if they legitimately don't deliver to your address.

What still isn't covered

The regulation doesn't apply to certain categories, including audiovisual content, transport services, financial services, and some healthcare services — areas governed by separate EU rules. It also doesn't prevent a trader from having genuinely different websites, prices, or terms for different countries for legitimate reasons unrelated to nationality or residence (such as differing VAT rates that must legally be applied based on the customer's location) — the ban targets discrimination based on where you are, not every price variation that happens to correlate with geography.

Dismantling forced redirects

If a site automatically redirects you to your home country's version without asking, look for a country selector, usually in the header or footer, and use it to opt back into the version you actually want to browse — the regulation specifically requires that any such redirection only happens with your explicit consent, and that you can access the originally requested version if you choose to.

If your card gets rejected for its country of issue

A payment declined purely because of the issuing country of your card — rather than a genuine security or fraud check — is a textbook geo-blocking violation. Document exactly what happened (screenshots of the error, the checkout flow, and ideally the payment method options shown) before raising a formal complaint.

Where to report a violation

Each EU member state designates a national enforcement body responsible for geo-blocking complaints, generally the same consumer protection authority handling other consumer law matters in that country. Persistent or large-scale violations — a retailer systematically segmenting the EU market by nationality — can draw significant penalties, since regulators treat this as a single-market integrity issue as much as an individual consumer complaint.

A practical checklist before you complain

Before assuming a violation, check: was the price difference actually based on your nationality/location, or a legitimate VAT/tax difference? Did the retailer refuse the sale outright, or just decline to ship (which may be legal on its own)? Was your card genuinely rejected for its country of issue, or for an unrelated fraud-prevention reason? Getting this distinction right up front makes your complaint far more credible if you do escalate it.
